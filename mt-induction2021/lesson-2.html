Panning and balance

  • Listen to this band recording.
  • Think about the panning and balance choices that have been made.
  • How many instruments can you hear? Is everything audible? What things are louder than others?
  • Can you hear any pairs of instruments? How have they been panned?

Similar tracks are often panned differently to increase separation.

Double tracking and stereo widening

Listen to this guitar sound. A delay has been added, and panned left, widening the stereo image a little.

  • Can you hear the reverb? What is interesting about the reverb here?
  • Can you see how powerful manipulating the stereo field can be?
  • How has stereo widening been used in this production to differentiate between vocal tracks that could otherwise sound very similar?
  • What other effects can you hear?

Usually, double tracking refers to doing two (or more!) takes of one part, one after the other. I.e. the two copies are actually separate recordings, but with the same mics, positioning, volume, etc. It takes a lot of skill to record a track the same twice.

Of course, there are tiny human errors (or slight musical differences, strumming, etc.). It is these errors and tiny differences between the two recordings that create the interesting stereo effect. Sometimes they are exactly in sync, sometimes not.

AC/DC use a lot of double-tracking and also panning layers of guitars to widen the stereo image and create a much thicker sound (as in the example at the top of this page)
